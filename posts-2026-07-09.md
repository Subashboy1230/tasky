## Theme: Access is not adoption. Adoption is not learning.

The sacred cow: the AI-adoption number. Districts and vendors celebrate "X% of students have used AI" as if availability equals progress. This week at ISTE, even Microsoft's own recap led with "access is just the beginning." A Stanford study of two districts puts a hard number on the gap: 2.18 minutes of weekly use.

### LinkedIn draft (copy-paste ready)

2.18 minutes.

That is how long the average student used an AI tutor per week in a Stanford study of two districts.

Not per session. Per week.

Even with scheduled time to open the tool, only 61% of students in one district and 53% in the other used it at all. The platform's own bar for measurable reading gains is 30 minutes a week.

This week at ISTELive 2026 in Orlando, the loudest signal in Microsoft's recap was a quiet one. Their first takeaway: access to AI tools is just the beginning.

We keep scoring the wrong thing. Licenses bought. Logins created. Tools deployed.

None of those are learning.

A district can hit 100% access and still get two minutes of real use per kid. Access is the easy half. Engaged, structured, supported practice is the half that actually moves a reading score.

Access is not adoption. Adoption is not learning.

At the Greater Dayton School, the number we watched was never logins. It was the minutes a student actually spent in productive practice, and what they could do six weeks later.

The metric was never "students have the tool." It is whether the tool ever earns 30 real minutes of a kid's attention.

## Theme 2 (alternate): AI literacy is not AI readiness

**Template:** Reframe-the-metric
**Fresh anchor:** EdTech Magazine, "ISTELive 26: What Does an AI-Ready Graduate Look Like?" by Amy McIntosh — verified byline `Jul 06 2026` (3 days old). https://edtechmagazine.com/k12/article/2026/07/istelive-26-what-does-ai-ready-graduate-look
Named source in article: ISTE+ASCD's expanded Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate; Joseph South, chief innovation officer, ISTE+ASCD.

The sacred metric to puncture: "AI literacy." For three years districts treated "we cover AI literacy" as the finish line. At ISTE last week, ISTE itself moved the line to production skills, six named roles, applied on day one of a job.

### LinkedIn draft (copy-paste ready)

AI literacy.

For three years that was the finish line. Teach kids how AI works, how to prompt it, how to spot a hallucination.

At ISTELive 2026 last week, ISTE moved the line.

Its expanded Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate defines six roles a student has to embody, not one concept they have to understand: Learner, Researcher, Synthesizer, Problem Solver, Connector, Storyteller.

Joseph South, chief innovation officer at ISTE+ASCD, put the gap plainly. Students, he said, are "expected to know how to use AI the day they show up."

Read that again. Not understand AI. Use it. On day one, in a real job.

AI literacy is knowing what the tool is. AI readiness is what you can build with it under deadline.

We spent three years teaching kids to explain the engine. The workplace is asking them to drive.

Literacy is the vocabulary. Readiness is the paragraph.

This is why "we cover AI literacy" is no longer a real answer for a district. Vocabulary was never the outcome. Fluent production under pressure is.

At EverTutor we build for the second bar, not the first. The win is not that a student can define what the model does. It is what they can make it do.
